The problem

Intro

This case study is part of the LBJ Documentation project

Pain Points

  • The documentation has too much text and isn’t visually appealing.
  • Long texts are hard to remember. Drivers tend to forget to do some critical tasks.
  • Some concepts are not easy to understand quickly without visual aids.
  • Drivers can’t dedicate too much time or attention to the content because they will access it through mobile and usually while working.

Audience

Adult drivers of any gender and any knowledge level.

The Solution

Design Decisions

  • Images are designed to fit a mobile screen, they have to be simple and clear.
  • The visuals must match the company’s tone, which is friendly, not too formal, and accessible even for beginners.
  • Characters represent the company drivers and clients, i.e., LBJ Drivers in Jacksonville, Florida, USA.
  • Visuals complement or accompany text but don’t necessarily replace it.

Tools

All the visuals were made in Figma to take advantage of its vector and component organization capabilities and of the possibility of updating elements with any free version. Components will be exported as SVG to ensure scalability.

Shapes and Symbols

Symbols were inspired by icons found in digital maps and apps.

Shapes try to be familiar and easy to interpret in small sizes, they aim to be obvious and unambiguous.

To avoid friction, in the documents, each element is presented by itself in a bigger size before using it in a small size.

Shapes

Simple and sharp images.

One color or the least possible.

Avoid too many parts or many different colors

Ambiguous minimalistic rounded shapes that are not easy to interpret.

Symbols

Dot: in a path represents an event or a location like a pick up, drop off or ride starting point.

Person: a person with different variants according to role and gender.

Pin: points to a relevant location to the ride, like pick up or drop off point. It can have different states to indicate what’s in that location, the passenger or a place. They’re also used as bullets in the learning path of training.

Solid Blue: is a portion that has been driven, it means it’s on the clock, it’s paired with the car.

Dashed Grey: is the pending or not used portion of the path.

Car: depending on the state will vary the content and shape.

Example: The driver is transporting the passenger. Ride started at the pickup point and it has not ended yet.

Composition

Different elements and annotations will explain a concept in a “scene.” Like with the smaller connections, spacing, sizing, and alignments are applied to make elements fully visible, legible, and their relationship to be clear.

Layout

Many different scenes can be displayed on one page, but only if they’re related, if they follow a logical order, and if the size of each allows them to share that space.

Layout

Do
  • Components are distributed along the page’s length.
  • Elements are fully visible.
  • Order is logical (ie: clock in, on clock, clock out).
Don't
  • Components are cluttered.
  • Some elements are not fully visible.
  • They don’t follow a logical order.

Conclusions

This is a work in progress, so there's still room for change and learning, but so far, I can say:

  • Testing each element in the screen where the visuals will be displayed is critical. You can get lost in details that have poor contrast or that you only see with a 1500% zoom. I had to discard many assets because I didn’t test them soon enough and didn’t know they'd look confusing or not even noticeable.
  • Shadows look great in design, but they might not render well. I decided not to use them when I realized that the visual benefits did not outweigh the extra workload of preparing the SVG files so they look good.
  • Something that looks realistic may not look good on screen. Some colors used in real life don't have good contrast, or the size is not big enough to be legible. A not-so-accurate representation might be faster to see and understand than a realistic imitation.
