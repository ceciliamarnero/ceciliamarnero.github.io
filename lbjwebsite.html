The Problem

About the client
  • LBJ Transportation USA is an NEMT (non-emergency medical transportation) family business based in Florida, USA.
  • The company’s mission is to drive passengers to their medical appointments so they get there on time, in a safe, respectful and caring way.
  • Clients hire the service usually through a healthcare insurance company but can pay out of pocket too. Insurance companies would hire LBJ usually through a broker.
The problem

LBJ had built a brand new website but had a critical issue: it didn't include any relevant content that would match the business description. Google judged it as a violation and suspended the account affecting the website’s ranking and hiding the company in Google Reviews and Google Maps, which seriously affects business trust and findability. Additionally, the website was not designed with a UX approach nor considered business needs, so it wasn’t useful for clients or the company.

Business Outcomes
  • Make website discoverable through Google Reviews and Maps again.
  • Make potential clients read Google reviews, and make clients leave reviews.
  • Get more calls. They feel more comfortable explaining and talking on the phone.
  • Have a website that reflects brand values and its family history.
Constraints
  • Company doesn’t promote their website in any other way but through Google but business account is suspended and needs the new version to appeal.
  • Client doesn't want to do any formal research with real users.
  • They haven’t worked with a UX designer in this project before.
  • It’s a small company. They don’t have too much personnel, time or budget to invest in the UX process.
Opportunities
  • I was the designer and the developer so we didn’t need detailed handoffs.
  • Shopify is a low-code tool, some basic components are ready to use or customizable through CSS and HTML.
  • I had been working for the company for some time, I knew company’s communication style and what image they wanted to convey.

Research

User interviews was not a possibility, so to know my audience needs and behavior, I investigated clients, callers (i.e. people that called to ask information but not necessarily get to use the service) and relevant demographics.

My main sources were:

Users

To know more about our potential users, I used basically 3 different sources of information:

  1. Clients & Callers questionnaire

    I asked:

    • What are their most common questions? Is there something not clear or particularly hard to understand for them?
    • What are their concerns? Are they worried about something? Do they express some emotion, like anxiety, stress, sadness or another?
    • Is the caller the person who needs the service (ie the passenger)? Or is it for a different person? How are they related?
    • What’s their gender and age.
    • Where did they find the phone number?
    • What time do they usually call?
    • Do they pay with their insurance or out of pocket? What’s their healthcare insurance company?
  2. Calls' Conversations analysis

    The SME was also the Dispatch operator. While we were in meetings, she would get calls from passengers or caregivers. At first they would interrupt our conversations, but turns out they became a reliable and practical source of information. I’d hear why they called, and through their voice I could even sense how they felt. Likewise, the SME would respond; and the tone of her voice, vocabulary, and care would tell me precisely how the company treat clients. I carried those insights to each step of the content creation.Demographics: I analyzed relevant stats to know more about potential users: disabilities, households’ incomes in the area, how households care for their elderlies, who makes the money decisions, and others.

Site Heuristics Evaluation

I ran a thorough analysis of the website current version, covering:

  • Content: Can Susan and Michael find the information they need? Can they find the site by using the terms they’d normally use in Google Search?
  • Information Architecture: can users find their way in the website to know what to do? Are labels, menus and buttons descriptive and accurate? Is information scannable?
  • Accessibility: are texts legible? Are contrasts acceptable (according to WCAG)?
  • SEO configuration: has configuration being set and optimized?
  • Images: are images clear? Are they descriptive and match with content?
Competitors’ Websites Analysis

Since the goals for a website like this one were not clear yet, I analyzed competitors’ websites. I was interested mainly on their content, what kind of information they show, the terminology and key aspects of the service they highlight.

Pain points

Now that I know more about the users, their needs and what the site currently offers, I can infer some issues:

  • Users can't find the reviews through Google Maps and this affects credibility. Sarah needs to know other people’s recommendations to hire a service that will take care of her father.
  • The task of finding an NEMT service for a loved one implies several unpleasant feelings. Sarah is under stress and needs information about the service and other people’s experiences using it, and yet, she might still have doubts.
  • Michael needs specifics about the service so he can evaluate if the service and company are reliable to handle a large volume of clients but the information is not there.
  • The content is not descriptive nor clear users won’t understand what’s the service about. Even images are confusing.
  • Users can’t read other people’s Google reviews nor testimonies in the website.
  • SEO website’s configuration is not set so Google can’t use it to show the name of company.
New Website Goals

This is what we want for the new version:

  1. The content to be clear enough so that Google can reindexe it and company can appeal violation.
  2. In order to brokers wants to partner, the website has to inform legal details and advantages of this company. We will know if it works when company gets more calls from brokers.
  3. In order to sell the service to eventual passengers or to their caregivers, we need to explain in plan English what the service is about, how the service can solve their problems, the reviews of previous clients and the phone number and form so they can ask more info. We will know if it works when company gets more calls from users.

Design Strategy

UX Outcomes
  1. We want users in the area to find the website through Google Search or Google Maps.
  2. We want users to understand and trust the service so they call to book the service or ask questions about the specifics of their particular needs.
  3. We want Sarah to call the healthcare company to ask them to hire this service for her dad.
  4. We want Michael to call the company to negotiate a partnership.
Design Process

It was urgent to come back right away to be discoverable through Google, so I decided to run a lean UX design process whose main strategy was:

  1. Develop MVP, plan and prioritize accordingly: content has priority over UI.
  2. Prefer fast low-fi wireframes to iterate faster. Prefer digital over paper to share remotely with stakeholders.
  3. Instead of high-fi wireframes, develop a working prototype in Shopify and iterate until is fully functional.
  4. Client didn’t want to involve users so we didn’t run tests, each iteration would be approved by them. User testing would be done once the whole website is complete.

Content Design

  • User should trust a sensitive issue on this service, and since brand hadn’t defined its voice, we made one that reflects company’s values and shapes a respectful and empathetic communication with users and clients. Voice is respectful, simple and kind.
  • Include keywords to solve Google penalty and to improve SEO to be discoverable so that users can find the service and read other clients’ reviews.
  • Include phone number prominently to let users call and speak if they need to have a private conversation, or if they don’t feel so comfortable using a website. This also obeys business goals.
  • Show Google Reviews with the possibility to add one so that users trust the service and recommend it to others.
  • Include a contact form so that users know that the company is open to know from them, wether is to ask questions or send comments or complaints. This could also deter unhappy customers from leaving complaints through a bad Google review.
  • Consider accessibility (good but not heavy images, good contrast, responsiveness, simple texts) to improve UX and SEO.
Detours. How I dealt with the unexpected

Not everything went as smooth as desired so I took some detours while focusing on our goals. Original plan was not discarded but put off until better conditions are given.

  1. Setback: The stakeholders had to get involved in other projects so many approvals and tasks got delayed. Detour: I created new versions to release the approved bit right away.
  2. Setback: The company decided not to get new pictures of the service with real clients so they provided some old ones but they were covered with text. Detour: I used AI to remove the text and we bought some stock images.
  3. Setback: The Shopify theme that the original site was using had some issues I didn’t foreseen, for example: you can’t add fields to the contact form. Detour: I had to drop the Subject field for the time being.

Takeaways

This is what I’ve learnt and what I’d advise to my future me to do next time:

  1. You could design and develop at the same time with fast low-code developer tools. You can start off from a rough prototype and iterate through a perfectly complete working app without complicated handoffs.
  2. Shopify is easy to use but is not a good fit if you sell services.
  3. Bad UX can still meets business critical needs. The first version was not good but did the job, the company got calls, user would find them through Google, read the reviews and hire the service. But when the new version went live, Google penalized the account and users couldn’t find them anymore.
  4. No matter how fast you can go, sometimes the pace will be set by the client. I'm patient and I expect delays.
  5. Client might not be able to do everything according to your plan. I get ready, I have a plan B and even C.
  6. ChatGPT can be confusing, to follow its suggestions can make you work for a company that does not even exist. I ask but check the sources and question everything. Use prompts with the constraints to respond to your specific needs.
  7. There’s so much the website can do for a business. The company should take care of their voice and image in every channel they interact with users. I can create great content for the website, but the image of the company depends on many factors out of your scope. Advise the client to be consistent. The website is not responsible for the entire company’s work.
